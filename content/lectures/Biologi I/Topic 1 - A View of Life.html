Topic 1 — A View of Life

The characteristics of life and the levels of biological organisation.

① Distinguish among the levels of biological organisation.

② Identify the basic characteristics of life.

1.1  The Characteristics of Life

Biology is the scientific study of life. Living things are hugely diverse, yet they are all built from the same chemical elements as non-living things and obey the same physical and chemical laws.

Six characteristics define a living organism:

1 · OrganisedBuilt in levels, atoms → biosphere.
2 · Need materials & energyTo stay alive and do work.
3 · Maintain homeostasisKeep internal conditions stable.
4 · Respond to stimuliReact to the environment.
5 · Reproduce & developMake more of their own kind.
6 · Have adaptationsSuited to their environment; evolve.

1 · Living things are organised

  • The cell is the basic unit of structure and function of all living things — organisms may be unicellular or multicellular.
  • Each level is more complex than the one before it. As complexity increases, each level gains new emergent properties (new, unique characteristics).

Levels of biological organisation (smallest → largest)

  • Atom — smallest unit of an element (electrons, protons, neutrons).
  • Molecule — two or more atoms joined.
  • Cell — structural & functional unit of life.
  • Tissue — cells with a common structure and function.
  • Organ — tissues working together for a task.
  • Organ system — several organs working together.
  • Organism — an individual living thing.
  • Population — organisms of the same species in one area.
  • Community — interacting populations in an area.
  • Ecosystem — a community plus its physical environment.
  • Biosphere — all regions of Earth inhabited by living things.

2 · Life requires materials and energy

  • Energy is the capacity to do work — needed to maintain organisation and run life processes.
  • Metabolism = all the chemical reactions in a cell.
  • The Sun is the ultimate energy source for nearly all life. Plants and algae capture solar energy through photosynthesis, converting it into the chemical energy of carbohydrates.
  • Ecosystems run on chemical cycling and energy flow:
    • Chemicals are recycled — they pass from producers → consumers → decomposers, then back to plants after death and decomposition.
    • Energy is not recycled — it flows one way through the food chain, so there must be constant input of solar energy.

3 · Living things maintain homeostasis

Homeostasis is keeping internal conditions within certain limits. Organisms must maintain biological balance; feedback systems monitor conditions and make adjustments.

4 · Living things respond to stimuli

  • Living things interact with and respond to changes in their environment — often producing movement (e.g. a plant bending toward the sun, a turtle withdrawing into its shell, birds migrating).
  • Responses help ensure survival and let the organism carry out its activities.

5 · Living things reproduce and develop

  • All organisms must reproduce to maintain a population. Reproduction = giving rise to another organism like itself.
  • The manner of reproduction varies between organisms.
  • Offspring receive copies of genetic information (genes), which determine the organism's characteristics.

6 · Living things have adaptations

  • An adaptation is any modification that makes an organism better able to function in its environment.
  • Diversity exists because, over long periods, organisms respond to changing environments by developing new adaptations.
  • Evolution = the change in a population of organisms over time to become better suited to the environment.
