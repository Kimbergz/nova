2.1  Chemistry of Water

Water is a polar molecule: oxygen pulls the shared electrons closer, so oxygen is partially negative and the two hydrogens are partially positive. This shape makes hydrogen bonding possible.

A hydrogen bond is a weak attraction between a slightly positive hydrogen atom and a slightly negative atom.

  • Can form between different molecules or within the same molecule.
  • A single hydrogen bond breaks easily, but many together are collectively strong.
  • Helps maintain the structure of complex molecules like proteins and DNA.

The five key properties of water

  • 1 · High heat capacity. Many hydrogen bonds let water absorb a lot of heat with little temperature change. Its temperature rises and falls slowly, helping organisms keep a stable internal temperature.
  • 2 · High heat of evaporation. Hydrogen bonds must be broken to evaporate water, so it is harder to evaporate than alcohol. Organisms cool when their heat is used to evaporate water (e.g. sweating, transpiration).
  • 3 · Good solvent. Polar water dissolves ionic salts and other polar substances (e.g. Na⁺ and Cl⁻ separate and become surrounded by water).
  • 4 · Cohesive and adhesive.
    • Cohesion — water molecules cling to each other (hydrogen bonding) → water flows freely, and gives surface tension.
    • Adhesion — water clings to other polar surfaces → capillary action.
    • Together they allow water transport in plants (roots → leaves) and in blood vessels.
  • 5 · Ice is less dense than liquid water. Below 4 °C hydrogen bonds become more rigid and open; water expands as it freezes at 0 °C, so ice floats. This insulates the water below — without it, oceans would freeze solid from the bottom up.

2.2  Organic Molecules

Organic molecules contain both carbon and hydrogen atoms. The four classes of organic molecules (biomolecules) in living things are: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids.

  • Most consist of repeating units. Each repeating unit is a monomer; a molecule made of many monomers is a polymer. (Example: amino acids join to form a protein.)
  • Lipids are not polymers — they contain two different types of subunits.

Synthesis & degradation reactions

Dehydration reaction (synthesis) — joins monomers into a polymer by forming a covalent bond; water is produced.
Monomers → Polymer + Water  (e.g. glucose → starch)
Hydrolysis reaction (degradation) — adds a water molecule to break a covalent bond, splitting a polymer into monomers.
Polymer + Water → Monomers  (e.g. digestion of starch → glucose)

Enzymes are required for cells to carry out these reactions. An enzyme speeds up a chemical reaction (a catalyst); it is not consumed or changed by the reaction.

2.3  Carbohydrates

Functions: energy source and structural building material. Contain carbon, hydrogen and oxygen in a 1:2:1 ratio. Three varieties:

  • Monosaccharides — a single (simple) sugar with a backbone of 3–7 carbons.
    • Hexoses (6 carbons): glucose (blood sugar), fructose (fruit sugar), galactose.
    • Pentoses (5 carbons): ribose and deoxyribose (found in nucleotides).
  • Disaccharides — two monosaccharides joined by dehydration synthesis.
    • Lactose (milk sugar) = galactose + glucose
    • Sucrose (table sugar) = glucose + fructose
    • Maltose = glucose + glucose
    Lactose-intolerant people lack the enzyme lactase, which breaks lactose into galactose and glucose.
  • Polysaccharides — polymers of many monosaccharides.
    • Starch — energy storage in plants.
    • Glycogen — energy storage in animals.
    • Cellulose — makes up plant cell walls.
    • Chitin — cell walls of fungi and exoskeletons of some animals.
    • Peptidoglycan — cell walls of bacteria.

2.4  Lipids

Large, nonpolar molecules that are insoluble in water and varied in structure. Functions: long-term energy storage, structural components, heat retention, cell communication & regulation, and protection. Varieties: fats, oils, phospholipids, steroids, waxes.

1 · Triglycerides (fats & oils)

  • Function: long-term energy storage and insulation.
  • Structure: one glycerol molecule linked to three fatty acids by dehydration synthesis.
  • Fatty acids are either:
    • Saturated — no double bonds between carbons; solid at room temperature (e.g. butter, lard).
    • Unsaturated — one or more double bonds; liquid at room temperature (e.g. plant oils). Groups can be on the same side (cis) or opposite sides (trans) of the double bond.
    • Trans — a triglyceride with at least one bond in a trans configuration.

2 · Phospholipids (membrane components)

  • Similar to triglycerides, but with one glycerol + two fatty acids + a modified phosphate group.
  • The fatty acid tails are nonpolar and hydrophobic; the phosphate head is polar and hydrophilic.
  • Function: form the plasma membranes of cells. In water they form a lipid bilayer — polar heads face the water, nonpolar tails face away and form a hydrophobic core. Kinks in the tails keep the membrane fluid.

3 · Steroids

  • Made of four fused carbon rings with various functional groups attached.
  • Function: component of animal cell membranes, and regulation.
  • Examples: cholesterol (precursor for other steroids; can also contribute to circulatory disorders), testosterone and estrogen (sex hormones that differ only in their functional groups).

4 · Waxes

  • Long-chain fatty acids joined to alcohol-containing carbon chains. Solid at room temperature, waterproof, resistant to breakdown.
  • Function: protection. Examples: earwax, plant cuticle, beeswax.

2.5  Proteins

Proteins are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide bonds (a covalent bond between amino acids). Up to 50% of a cell's dry weight is protein.

  • Two or more amino acids = a peptide; long chains = polypeptides.
  • A protein is a polypeptide folded into a particular shape — essential for it to work.
  • There are 20 common amino acids, differing by their R (variable) group.

Six functions of proteins

  • Metabolism — most enzymes are proteins that speed up reactions.
  • Support — structural proteins such as keratin and collagen.
  • Transport — membrane channel/carrier proteins; hemoglobin carries oxygen.
  • Defense — antibodies bind antigens to protect cells.
  • Regulation — hormones influence cell metabolism.
  • Motion — microtubules move cell parts; actin & myosin let muscles contract.

Four levels of protein structure

  • Primary — the linear sequence of amino acids. Changing the sequence produces a different protein.
  • Secondary — alpha helices and beta (pleated) sheets held by hydrogen bonds.
  • Tertiary — the overall 3-D shape, stabilised by hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen, ionic and covalent bonds.
  • Quaternary — more than one polypeptide joined together.
Denaturation: a protein cannot work unless it folds into its proper shape. When it loses that shape it is denatured — caused by certain chemicals, a change in pH, or high temperature.

2.6  Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides. Two varieties:

  • DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) — genetic material that stores information for its own replication and for the sequence of amino acids in proteins. Sugar: deoxyribose.
  • RNA (ribonucleic acid) — performs many functions including protein synthesis and gene regulation. Sugar: ribose.

Structure of a nucleotide

Each nucleotide has three parts: a phosphate group, a pentose sugar, and a nitrogenous base.

TypeBases
Pyrimidines (single ring)Cytosine, Thymine (DNA only), Uracil (RNA only)
Purines (double ring)Adenine, Guanine

DNA contains adenine, guanine, cytosine, thymine. RNA contains adenine, guanine, cytosine, uracil. Nucleotides join by dehydration synthesis into a strand.

Structure of DNA vs RNA

  • The backbone is alternating sugar–phosphate molecules.
  • RNA is usually single-stranded; DNA is double-stranded, held together by hydrogen bonds between bases and twisted into a double helix.
  • Complementary base pairing: Adenine pairs with Thymine; Cytosine pairs with Guanine. So the number of purines (A+G) always equals the number of pyrimidines (T+C).

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)

  • A nucleotide made of adenine + ribose (= adenosine) + three phosphates.
  • A high-energy molecule because the last two phosphate bonds are unstable and easily broken.
  • Hydrolysis of the terminal phosphate bond yields: ADP (adenosine diphosphate) + an inorganic phosphate (P) + energy to do cellular work.
