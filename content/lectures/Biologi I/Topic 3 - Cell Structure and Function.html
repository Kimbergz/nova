3.1  The Cell: Basic Unit of Life

The Cell Theory

  • Cells are the basic units of structure/organisation and function in organisms.
  • All cells come from pre-existing cells (cells are self-reproducing).

Developed by Schleiden (1838) & Schwann (1839): all plants and animals consist of cells. Virchow (1855): new cells form by division of existing cells. Weismann (1880): all cells today trace back through shared structures and functions.

Cell sizes vary but are mostly microscopic. Some cells move (e.g. blood cells); some stay fixed (e.g. muscle cells); some divide quickly (e.g. skin cells); some rarely divide (e.g. nerve cells).

3.2  Prokaryotic & Eukaryotic Cells

Main difference: a prokaryotic cell lacks a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; a eukaryotic cell has both.

Prokaryotes

Lack a membrane-bound nucleus (simpler than eukaryotes). All are unicellular. Placed in two domains:

Cell wallLacks peptidoglycanHas peptidoglycan
Fun factLive in extreme, harsh conditionsMost diverse & widespread group
  • Cell envelope = plasma membrane (lipid bilayer with proteins; can form mesosomes to increase surface area) + cell wall (keeps shape, strengthened by peptidoglycan) + glycocalyx (polysaccharide layer).
  • Cytoplasm — semifluid solution with water, molecules and enzymes. Contains the nucleoid (region holding the single, circular DNA), plasmids (small extra rings of DNA), and ribosomes (make proteins).
  • External structures — flagella (movement), fimbriae (bristle-like fibres), and conjugation pili (pass DNA between cells).

Eukaryotes

  • Have a membrane-bound nucleus that houses DNA.
  • Have specialised, compartmentalised organelles that perform specific functions and isolate reactions from each other.
  • Plasma membrane (phospholipid bilayer with proteins) separates cell contents from the environment and regulates what passes in and out.
  • Have a cytoskeleton (supporting framework) to maintain shape and transport materials.

Origin of organelles

  • Nucleus — formed when the plasma membrane folded inward (invaginated) and surrounded the DNA with a double membrane.
  • Endomembrane system — formed by proliferation of membrane, increasing surface area for transport (includes ER and Golgi).
  • Endosymbiotic theory — mitochondria and chloroplasts came from smaller prokaryotes engulfed by a larger eukaryotic ancestor. The host benefited from their ability to use oxygen (mitochondria) or do photosynthesis (chloroplasts); over time they lived cooperatively inside the host.

Evidence for the endosymbiotic theory

  • Mitochondria and chloroplasts are similar to bacteria in size and structure.
  • Both are surrounded by a double membrane.
  • Both contain a small amount of their own genetic material and divide by splitting.
  • Both contain their own ribosomes and make some proteins.
  • Their ribosomal RNA sequences suggest a prokaryotic origin.

3.3  The Nucleus & Ribosomes

The Nucleus

  • Command centre of the cell, usually near the centre.
  • Nuclear envelope — double membrane separating nuclear contents from cytoplasm; nuclear pores allow exchange between them.
  • Contains chromatin (DNA + RNA + protein) in semifluid nucleoplasm. Chromatin condenses into chromosomes during cell division — the carriers of genetic information.
  • Most nuclei have a nucleolus, which synthesises rRNA.

Ribosomes

  • Site of protein synthesis; composed of rRNA, with a large and a small subunit (made in the nucleolus). Contain enzymes that form peptide bonds.
  • Located on the endoplasmic reticulum (making it "rough"), or free in the cytoplasm (singly or in groups called polyribosomes).

3.4  Membranous Organelles

Two classes of organelles:

  • Endomembrane system — organelles that communicate via membrane channels and small vesicles (nuclear membrane, ER, Golgi, lysosomes, vesicles).
  • Energy-related organelles — mitochondria and chloroplasts (independent and self-sufficient).

Endomembrane system

A network of organelles exchanging materials through membrane-enclosed transport vesicles. Each vesicle has membrane proteins that act as routing signals to guide it to the right destination. Consists of the nuclear envelope, ER, Golgi apparatus and vesicles.

Rough ERSmooth ER
Studded with ribosomes. Synthesises proteins and adds sugar to form glycoproteins. Forms transport vesicles that move to the Golgi. No ribosomes. Synthesises lipids (phospholipids & cholesterol for the cell membrane), breaks down toxins, and forms transport vesicles.

Golgi complex

  • Stacks of flattened membranous sacs called cisternae. Processes, sorts and modifies proteins.
  • Three areas: cis face (entry), trans face (exit), and the medial region in between.
  • Pathway: transport vesicles carry glycoprotein to the Golgi cis face → modified in the Golgi → packaged into vesicles at the trans face → sent to the plasma membrane.

Lysosomes

  • Membrane-bound vesicles in animal cells. Contain powerful, highly acidic digestive enzymes.
  • Fuse with incoming vesicles and digest macromolecules (and even cell parts) into simpler subunits.

Secretory vesicles carry products to the membrane, where exocytosis releases them as secretions.

Did you know? Specialised cells have more or fewer copies of an organelle depending on their job — e.g. liver cells (which detoxify drugs) have lots of smooth ER; nerve cells have lots of plasma membrane.

Vacuoles

  • Enclosed membranous sacs, like plant "lysosomes". Plant cells have a large central vacuole (up to 90% of cell volume).
  • Functions: store water, nutrients, pigments and waste; develop turgor pressure that provides structural support; important for plant growth.

Microbodies (e.g. Peroxisomes)

  • Contain specialised enzymes. Peroxisomes transfer hydrogen from compounds to oxygen, break down fatty acids, synthesise phospholipids, and detoxify (e.g. degrade alcohol in liver/kidney cells; convert stored fats to sugars in plant seeds).

Energy-producing organelles

Mitochondria and chloroplasts convert energy from one form to another (into usable forms like ATP). Both have their own ribosomes and DNA (supporting the endosymbiosis theory).

Chloroplasts use sunlight to make carbohydrates, which the mitochondria then use; mitochondria produce CO₂ and H₂O, which the chloroplasts use — a continuous cycle.

3.5  The Cytoskeleton

Contains microfilaments, intermediate filaments and microtubules.

  • Functions: maintain cell shape, assist movement of the cell and organelles, and make internal transport possible.
  • It is dynamic — it rearranges its protein components as needed in response to internal and external changes.
ComponentRole
MicrofilamentsMuscle contraction (main contractile component) and animal cell division.
MicrotubulesRadiate from the centrosome; present in centrioles, cilia and flagella. Act as internal "tracks" along which vesicles and organelles move.
Intermediate filamentsMade of keratin; give great mechanical strength to skin cells.

Quick recall

The cell is one connected system. Example: the nucleus houses the genetic material and communicates with ribosomes in the cytoplasm. Products are moved around the cell by vesicles, which travel along the cytoskeletal network.
