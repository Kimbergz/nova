4.1  Structure & Function

The plasma membrane is a thin (about 7–10 nm), flexible, selectively permeable boundary that surrounds every cell. It controls everything that enters and leaves — essential for cell survival.

The Fluid Mosaic Model

  • The membrane is a phospholipid bilayer with proteins and other molecules embedded in it.
  • Fluid — lipids and proteins can move sideways (laterally) within the membrane.
  • Mosaic — many different types of molecules are embedded in the bilayer.
  • Held together by weak hydrophobic interactions. Lateral movement is frequent; flip-flop (a molecule flipping to the other layer) is rare, because the hydrophilic head would have to cross the hydrophobic core.

Four core functions

  • Separation & protection — separates the cell from its surroundings; essential for the origin of life.
  • Transport & communication — membrane proteins transport materials, transmit information, and act as enzymes.
  • Cell adhesion — adhesion molecules connect cells into tissues.
  • Homeostasis — regulates what enters and leaves, keeping the internal environment stable.

The phospholipid bilayer

Phospholipids are amphipathic (have both a water-loving and a water-avoiding part), so they naturally form a bilayer in water:

  • Hydrophilic head — a negatively-charged phosphate group; faces the watery environment (extracellular fluid and cytoplasm).
  • Hydrophobic tails — two fatty acid chains; repel water and face inward.
  • Their cylindrical shape favours a flat bilayer (✓) rather than a spherical micelle (✗).

The bilayer is flexible (changes shape without breaking), self-sealing (rounds up into closed vesicles), and can fuse with other bilayers to transfer materials or secrete products.

Other lipids & cholesterol — membrane fluidity

  • In cold temperatures, some organisms increase the proportion of unsaturated fatty acids in their membrane lipids.
  • Unsaturated tails have kinks at their double bonds that prevent tight packing → keep the membrane fluid. Saturated tails pack closely → make the membrane more viscous.

Membrane proteins

Two positions:

  • Integral membrane proteins — amphipathic proteins firmly bound to the membrane. Transmembrane proteins are integral proteins that extend all the way through.
  • Peripheral membrane proteins — sit on the inner or outer surface, bound to exposed parts of integral proteins (do not penetrate the bilayer).
  • Lipid-anchored proteins — covalently attached to a lipid in one layer; do not span the membrane.

Six functions of membrane proteins

  • Channel protein — lets molecules/ions pass through a channel. e.g. aquaporin, calcium channel.
  • Carrier protein — binds the substance and helps it across, changing shape. e.g. sodium–potassium pump.
  • Cell recognition protein — glycoproteins/glycolipids on the outer surface act as ID tags (carbohydrate chains under 15 sugars) that help the body recognise foreign substances. e.g. MHC glycoproteins.
  • Receptor protein — binds specific signal molecules so the cell can respond. e.g. hormone receptor.
  • Enzymatic protein — carries out metabolic reactions directly. e.g. ATP synthase.
  • Junction protein — forms junctions that attach adjacent cells. e.g. desmosome (animal), plasmodesmata (plant).

Selective permeability

The membrane lets some substances through but not others:

  • Small, non-charged molecules (CO₂, O₂, glycerol, alcohol) pass freely, following the concentration gradient.
  • Water crosses through a special protein called aquaporin, which speeds up water transport.
  • Polar molecules, charged molecules and ions are blocked from passing directly.

4.2  Passive Transport

Passive transport moves molecules down a concentration gradient (high → low) and does not require energy (ATP). Three examples: simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and osmosis.

Diffusion = movement of molecules down a concentration gradient. The rate depends on the particles' size & shape, their electric charge, and the temperature.

Simple diffusionFacilitated diffusion
Small non-polar molecules move directly through the phospholipid bilayer. e.g. O₂, CO₂. Polar molecules and ions move through channel or carrier proteins. e.g. glucose, amino acids, Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻.

Facilitated diffusion uses transport proteins that are specific (each carries only certain molecules). A carrier protein binds the molecule, undergoes a conformational change in shape to move it across, then resets — all without using ATP.

Osmosis

  • A special case of diffusion focused on the movement of water (the solvent), not the solute.
  • Diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane: water moves from where solute is low (high water) toward where solute is high (low water). Water can cross both ways, but the solute cannot.
  • Osmotic pressure is the pressure that develops due to osmosis.

Tonicity — how solutions affect cells

Tonicity = the relative solute concentrations of two fluids separated by a membrane. It tells us whether a cell will swell, shrink, or stay the same.

SolutionMeaningAnimal cellPlant cell
IsotonicEqual solute inside & out; no net water movement.Normal shape.Flaccid (normal).
HypotonicLower solute outside → water enters, cell swells.Swells, may burst (lysis).Becomes turgid (firm) from turgor pressure.
HypertonicHigher solute outside → water leaves, cell shrinks.Shrinks (crenation).Plasmolysis (shrinks away from wall).

Protozoans in fresh water (hypotonic) use contractile vacuoles to pump out excess water.

Real-life examples

  • Hypotonic: drinking too much pure water can swell/burst red blood cells; medical IV fluids (e.g. 0.45% saline) gently hydrate cells; soaking dried beans/fruit plumps them up; celery/carrots in fresh water become crisp again.
  • Isotonic: IV fluids (e.g. 0.9% saline) match blood's tonicity to avoid damaging cells.
  • Hypertonic: salting/curing meat & fish preserves food by drawing water out of bacteria; drinking sea water worsens dehydration; soy sauce shrinks red blood cells; gargling warm salt water reduces throat swelling; heavy sweating removes water from cells.

4.3  Active Transport

Active transport moves molecules/ions against the concentration gradient (low → high) using energy (ATP) and specific carrier proteins. It is vital for maintaining gradients, cell signalling and homeostasis.

1 · Sodium–Potassium Pump

  • Uses ATP to pump Na⁺ out of the cell and K⁺ in, against their gradients.
  • For every 3 Na⁺ pumped out, 2 K⁺ are pumped in.
  • This creates membrane polarisation, a membrane potential, and electrochemical gradients that store energy to drive other transport systems.

Bulk transport (via vesicles)

Large materials move in or out of the cell packaged inside vesicles.

2 · Exocytosis — release OUT of the cell

  • An intracellular vesicle fuses with the plasma membrane to release its contents outside. Used to secrete hormones, neurotransmitters, digestive enzymes, and to remove waste.
  • The vesicle membrane becomes part of the plasma membrane (both are nonpolar). Vesicles are produced by the Golgi body.
  • Steps: vesicle moves to the membrane → docks and fuses → contents released outside → vesicle membrane becomes part of the plasma membrane.

3 · Endocytosis — bringing INTO the cell

The membrane engulfs substances into a pouch that pinches off to form a vesicle inside the cell. Three ways:

TypeWhat it takes in
Phagocytosis ("cell eating")Large, solid particles (e.g. bacteria, debris). White blood cells engulf debris or viruses this way.
Pinocytosis ("cell drinking")Liquid or very small particles.
Receptor-mediated endocytosisA specific form of pinocytosis using receptor proteins and coated pits to selectively take in specific substances.

General steps: substance binds the membrane/receptor → membrane folds in to form a pouch → pouch pinches off into a vesicle → vesicle moves into the cell → a lysosome fuses with it and releases digestive enzymes.

4.4  Modification of Cell Surfaces

Extracellular Matrix (ECM) — animal cells

The ECM is a meshwork of proteins and polysaccharides just outside the cell that produced it. It provides structural support, cell adhesion, communication and tissue integrity. Components:

  • Collagen — provides tensile strength and structural support.
  • Elastin — provides elasticity; lets tissues stretch and recoil.
  • Fibronectin — glycoprotein that helps cells stick to the ECM and to each other.
  • Integrin — transmembrane receptor proteins that connect the ECM to the cytoskeleton inside the cell.
  • Proteoglycans — core protein with attached carbohydrate chains; provide hydration and resistance to compression.

Cell junctions — animal cells

Specialised connections between adjacent cells that let them behave in a coordinated way.

Plant cell surfaces

  • Plasmodesmata — cytoplasmic channels through the cell walls for cell-to-cell communication and transport. Allow water, ions, small solutes, RNA and proteins to pass (passive for small molecules; regulated for large/polar ones). Found in plant cells only.
  • Cuticle — waxy, non-living layer covering the epidermis, made of cutin; reduces water loss and protects against pathogens.
  • Cell wall — rigid layer outside the plasma membrane, made mainly of cellulose (plus hemicellulose and pectin); provides shape, support and protection.
  • Middle lamella — thin layer between adjacent cell walls, made mainly of pectin; acts as a "cement" holding cells together.
